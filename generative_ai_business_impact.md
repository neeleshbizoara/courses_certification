# The Business Impact of Generative AI

## Introduction

Generative AI has diverse applications that extend far beyond chatbots. This transformative technology is already reshaping how businesses operate, create, and make decisions.

---

## What is generative AI?

Generative AI is a type of artificial intelligence that can **create new content and ideas**, including:

- Text
- Images
- Music
- Code

---

## Using generative AI: three core capabilities

### 1) Create — generate new content

Generative AI can produce new content across many formats.

#### Text content

- Write articles, emails, and social media posts
- Generate text responses to customer service inquiries using [Conversational AI](https://cloud.google.com/conversational-ai?hl=en)
- Draft emails to clients using [Gemini in Gmail](https://support.google.com/mail/answer/13955415?sjid=11171800120465085432-NA)

#### Images, videos, and audio

- Create custom images for presentations using [Gemini in Google Slides](https://support.google.com/docs/answer/14355071?hl=en)
- Generate multiple design concepts for marketing materials (website pages, ads) using [Imagen](https://cloud.google.com/vertex-ai/generative-ai/docs/image/overview)
- Create video tutorials to guide customers through troubleshooting steps using [Google Vids](https://workspace.google.com/products/vids/)

#### Code in various programming languages

- Generate unit tests and other test cases — reduces manual testing effort while ensuring code works as expected
- Generate code as a starting point for larger software development projects using [Gemini Code Assist](https://cloud.google.com/products/gemini/code-assist?e=48754805)

## Multimodal generative AI

Gen AI can process and integrate information from **diverse formats** — plain text, images, videos, audio, and PDFs.

This capability is known as **multimodal gen AI**.

Models can:
- Process various input types individually
- Combine multiple kinds of data simultaneously

### Real-world multimodal examples

#### Text + image

A marketing team uses gen AI to create a series of social media posts (text) with accompanying images that align with their brand guidelines, including captions, hashtags, and optimal posting times.

#### Text + PDF

A legal team uses gen AI to summarize a lengthy legal contract (PDF) and answer specific questions about its clauses and implications, identifying potential risks and suggesting alternative language.

#### Text + video + data

A market research firm uses gen AI to analyze customer sentiment in video testimonials, the spoken words (text), and survey data collected alongside the video. This helps identify key themes, pain points, and overall customer satisfaction.

#### Sensor data + video

A manufacturing team enhances worker safety with gen AI. The AI analyzes real-time camera feeds and sensor data to identify potential hazards — such as spills or equipment malfunctions — and immediately alerts workers to the danger.

## Foundations of gen AI

## Introduction

Let's hear from the course creators about foundation models, how they're trained, and how they are being used to solve problems.

Generative AI starts with **foundation models**.

Foundation models are large AI models trained on enormous datasets. This broad training helps them develop a deep understanding of data and adapt to many downstream tasks.

Understanding foundation models is important because they represent a major shift in AI. They can streamline processes, automate tasks, and unlock new possibilities across many business functions.

---

### Where foundation models fit in AI

Artificial intelligence (AI) is when computer systems perform tasks that usually require human intelligence, such as understanding language or making decisions.

This is often achieved through machine learning (ML), where systems learn patterns from data to solve problems.

Traditionally, AI models were trained for one specific purpose using focused datasets (for example, spam detection). Foundation models are different: they are trained on massive and diverse data (text, images, code, audio, video), allowing one model to support many tasks.

That flexibility is what makes foundation models so groundbreaking.

### Foundation models vs LLMs

- **Foundation model**: Broad, general-purpose model trained on massive multimodal or domain-rich data.
- **LLM (Large Language Model)**: A specialized type of foundation model focused on language tasks.

So, LLMs are part of the foundation-model family.

---

## Key features of foundation models

### 1) Trained on diverse data

Foundation models are trained on a wide variety of data, helping them learn general patterns and relationships that transfer well across tasks.

### 2) Flexible

One model can support many use cases instead of building and maintaining a separate model for each task.

### 3) Adaptable

Foundation models can be further specialized for particular domains or use cases through additional targeted training and tuning.

---

## Examples of foundation models

### Gemini

Trained on a massive dataset of text, images, code, audio, video, and more. This multimodal training allows Gemini to perform tasks across many domains, including language understanding, image reasoning, and code generation.

### Imagen

Trained primarily on large datasets of images and text descriptions. Imagen can generate high-quality images from prompts, perform image editing tasks, and interpret image content.

### Chirp

Trained on large multilingual audio datasets. Chirp is designed for speech recognition and can support use cases such as voice assistants, transcription, and translation.

## Use case: Gemini in action

Morrisons, one of the UK's largest supermarkets, is delivering faster and more convenient shopping experiences with Google Cloud.

Using **BigQuery**, **Vertex AI**, and **Gemini**, Morrisons developed **Product Finder** — an in-app AI tool that helps customers instantly find products in-store.

Shoppers can type a product name and receive real-time location details (aisle and shelf area), even when layouts are updated.

### Real business impact

- Product Finder recorded about **50,000 hits per day** during Easter 2025.
- Usage continued growing as customers reused the tool on return shopping trips.
- Customers reported a less stressful in-store experience during high-pressure seasons (for example, holiday shopping).

---

### Problem Morrisons was solving

In large stores, even common products can be hard to locate quickly.

Morrisons wanted to provide customers with an in-store search experience similar to online shopping.

---

### How the solution works

Morrisons already had location data in **BigQuery** describing where products are in each store.

The AI flow:

1. Customer enters a natural-language query in the app
2. **Gemini** interprets the query and maps it to product and location identifiers
3. **Vertex AI** orchestrates inference and integration
4. **BigQuery** provides the precise product-location data
5. App returns aisle/location guidance to the customer

This architecture helped a relatively small team deliver production value quickly.

## Prompting models

### How are foundation models used for gen AI?

Foundation models are trained on vast amounts of data. To use that knowledge, you provide an input called a **prompt**.

Like other AI models, foundation models take in prompts and produce outputs.

What makes foundation models different is flexibility:

- Many traditional AI models have strict input formats (for example, only numbers, emails, or images)
- Foundation models — especially multimodal ones — can handle a much broader range of prompt types

---

## Prompting inputs and outputs

It is easiest to understand prompting with examples:

| Input (prompt) | Output |
|---|---|
| Question | The answer to the question |
| Request for an image based on a text description | The image |
| Request for a summary of a file | The file summary |
| Sample code with errors | Corrected code with no errors |

The possible prompt types are extensive. Depending on the foundation model, prompts can span text, code, images, audio, and combinations of these.

---

## Prompting as a skill

Designing effective prompts is both an art and a science.

You may hear this called:

- Prompting
- Prompt engineering
- Prompt design

Prompting is one of the most important skills for maximizing the value of gen AI models and tools.

---

## Key takeaway

Foundation models are powerful tools that are accessed through prompting. By crafting effective prompts, you can unlock their potential to generate text, translate languages, write creative content, and provide informative answers.

---

## One-line summary

**Prompting is the main way to use foundation models: better prompts lead to more accurate, useful, and business-ready outputs.**

## Building a successful gen AI strategy

## Introduction

Building a successful gen AI strategy requires both:

- A **top-down vision** from leadership
- A **bottom-up flow of ideas** from teams closest to daily work

The strongest outcomes happen when these two perspectives are aligned with clear business priorities.

---

## Top-down and bottom-up strategy

### Executives and high-level management

Executive sponsorship and a clear vision are critical for successful gen AI implementation.

Leaders should ensure that gen AI initiatives:

- Align with overall business goals
- Receive adequate funding and resources
- Gain organization-wide support
- Include safe spaces for teams to experiment and learn

When leadership provides direction and support, AI initiatives are more likely to move from pilot to real business value.

---

### Mid-level managers and individual contributors (ICs)

Mid-level managers and ICs are closest to daily operations and end-user needs — this gives them invaluable insight into where gen AI can help most.

Their role is to:

- Identify high-impact, feasible use cases in real workflows
- Champion adoption in teams and functions
- Encourage practical experimentation and fast feedback loops
- Integrate gen AI tools into existing processes to improve productivity and innovation

This bottom-up input ensures AI initiatives solve real problems, not just theoretical ones.

---

## How to align both approaches

Use this simple operating model:

1. **Leadership sets strategic priorities** (what outcomes matter most)
2. **Teams propose workflow-level use cases** (where AI can help immediately)
3. **Pilot quickly, measure impact, and iterate**
4. **Scale only what works** across departments

This creates a repeatable system for turning AI ideas into measurable business outcomes.

---

## Key takeaway

Successful gen AI strategy combines top-down executive sponsorship with bottom-up experimentation from teams, ensuring initiatives are both strategically aligned and operationally practical.

---

## One-line summary

**Gen AI strategy succeeds when leadership sets direction, teams drive practical use cases, and both align to business goals with measurable results.**

---

## Implementing a multi-directional strategy

Successful gen AI implementation requires a combination of top-down and bottom-up approaches. Both levels should be strategizing across six dimensions:

1. Strategic focus
2. Exploration
3. Responsible AI
4. Resourcing
5. Impact
6. Continuous improvement

---

### 1) Strategic focus

**Executives and high-level management**
- Focus on a specific area where gen AI can have a significant impact, then expand to other areas after initial success
- Prioritize use cases that are **feasible, actionable, affordable**, and have high anticipated business value and ROI

**Mid-level managers and ICs**
- Identify the most pressing pain points in daily work or those heard directly from customers — these are the best starting points for high-impact AI use cases
- Prioritize understanding user needs, workflows, and expectations (human-centered design)
- Explore use cases in **low-risk areas first** to allow for valuable learning and experimentation before scaling

---

### 2) Exploration

**Executives and high-level management**
- Empower employees to experiment with gen AI and identify use cases — for example, through hackathons or dedicated exploration time
- Foster a collaborative environment where teams share findings, insights, and best practices

**Mid-level managers and ICs**
- Experiment with different gen AI tools to learn what works and what doesn't
- Share findings with your team and leadership — if initial experiments succeed, expand scope and bring insights upward

---

### 3) Responsible AI

**Executives and high-level management**

## AI, ML, and generative AI: how they differ

### Conversation recap (simple explanation)

Artificial intelligence (AI), machine learning (ML), and generative AI are related — but they are not the same thing.

- **AI** is the big umbrella: building systems that perform tasks that usually require human intelligence (learning, reasoning, decision-making).
- **ML** is one common way to build AI: systems learn patterns from data to perform specific tasks.
- **Generative AI** is a specific application area (within ML/AI) focused on creating new content such as text, images, code, audio, or music.

---

### How AI systems work at a high level

At a simplified level, AI systems use a **model**.

- You provide an input
- The model processes the input using learned parameters
- You receive an output

The model is learned from data. Different data and training approaches produce different model behavior.

So even for non-technical leaders, understanding data quality and relevance is critical.

---

### Traditional AI use cases vs gen AI use cases

- Traditional predictive/classification AI often answers:  
  *"What is this?"* or *"What will happen?"*  
  (example: spam vs non-spam)

- Generative AI answers:  
  *"Can you create something new based on what you've learned?"*

Generative AI models learn underlying structures and patterns in very large datasets, then generate new content that is similar in style/pattern — but newly created.

---

### Analogy

Think of teaching a child to paint:

- **Traditional AI**: Teach the child how to judge whether a painting is good.
- **Generative AI**: Show many paintings, then ask the child to create a new one using what they learned.

## How ML models use data and why quality matters

ML models analyze the data they are given, identify patterns, and then estimate the likelihood of different outcomes when presented with new information.

Because of this, **data quality and data quantity** are critical to model performance.

### Five key data-quality factors

#### 1) Accuracy

If data is inaccurate, the model learns incorrect patterns and makes faulty predictions.

Analogy: teaching a child animals with mislabeled pictures leads to wrong understanding.

#### 2) Completeness

Completeness refers to both:

- dataset size
- coverage within the dataset

Models need enough examples to generalize well. Predicting weather from only one day of history is much weaker than using a large historical dataset.

#### 3) Representative data

Data should be inclusive and representative of real-world conditions.

If key demographics are missing, models can produce skewed or biased outcomes.

#### 4) Consistency

Inconsistent formats, labels, or conventions can confuse model training.

Analogy: a puzzle with mixed labeling styles (numbers on some pieces, letters on others) becomes harder to assemble correctly.

#### 5) Relevance

Training data must be relevant to the target task.

Example: traffic data from London is unlikely to help predict crop yields in Kansas.

---

## Key takeaway

High-performing ML depends on high-quality data: it must be accurate, complete, representative, consistent, and relevant to the business problem.

---

## One-line summary

**ML models are only as good as the data they learn from — strong data quality directly drives strong model outcomes.**

## Types of learning

### Machine learning approaches and data requirements

Data quality, accessibility, and form are foundational — but how that data is actually *used* for ML depends on the learning method.

ML has three primary learning approaches:

| Approach | Data type needed |
|---|---|
| Supervised learning | Labeled data |
| Unsupervised learning | Unlabeled data |
| Reinforcement learning | Interaction and feedback signals |

---

## Labeled versus unlabeled data

### Labeled data

Labeled data has tags — such as a name, type, or number — that assign meaning to the raw information.

Labels may be applied manually or by automated systems.

**Examples:**
- An image dataset where each picture is labeled as "cat" or "dog"
- Customer reviews labeled as "positive", "negative", or "neutral"

Labels allow algorithms to learn relationships between inputs and outputs, enabling accurate predictions on new data.

### Unlabeled data

Unlabeled data is raw, unprocessed data with no tags or assigned meaning.

**Examples:**
- An unorganized collection of photos
- A stream of audio recordings
- Website traffic logs without user categories

When working with unlabeled data, algorithms must discover patterns and structures independently — no labels to guide the process.

---

## Supervised machine learning

Supervised ML trains a model on **labeled data** where each input is paired with its correct output.

The model learns the relationship between inputs and outputs, then uses that relationship to predict outputs for new, unseen inputs.

**Example: predicting house prices**

Each record in the dataset includes:
- House size
- Number of bedrooms
- Location
- Actual sale price (the label)

The model learns the relationship between house features and price. Once trained, it can predict the price of a new house it has never seen.

---

## Unsupervised learning

Unsupervised ML works with **unlabeled data** to find natural groupings and hidden patterns.

There are no correct answers to learn from — the model explores on its own.

**Example: customer segmentation**

An algorithm analyzes customer purchase history and identifies clusters of customers with similar buying patterns — even without explicit categories having been defined in advance.

This is useful for:
- Targeted marketing
- Product recommendations
- Exploratory data analysis

Think of it as telling the model: "Here is the data — find what is interesting."

---

## Reinforcement learning

Reinforcement learning teaches a model through **interaction and feedback** rather than from a fixed dataset.

The model learns by:
- Taking actions in an environment
- Receiving positive feedback (reward) for good outcomes
- Receiving negative feedback (penalty) for bad outcomes
- Repeating and refining over many iterations

**Analogy:** Training a pet — reward good behavior, discourage bad behavior, and over time the pet learns what you want.

**Real-world examples:**
- Self-driving cars learning to navigate complex traffic
- Robots optimizing performance in a manufacturing plant
- Game-playing agents learning to win through trial and error

Reinforcement learning is especially useful when you cannot provide labeled training data or explicit instructions.

---

## Key takeaway

ML has three core learning paradigms: supervised (labeled data, predicts known outputs), unsupervised (unlabeled data, discovers hidden patterns), and reinforcement (feedback-driven, learns to maximize rewards). Choosing the right approach depends on your data and your goal.

---

## One-line summary

**Supervised learning predicts from labels, unsupervised learning discovers hidden structure, and reinforcement learning improves through trial and reward.**
